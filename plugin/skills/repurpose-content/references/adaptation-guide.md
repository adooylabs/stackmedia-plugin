# Content Adaptation Guide

## The Translation Loss Problem

Every platform has a native language — not just a format, but a set of cultural expectations about pacing, polish, tone, and relationship between creator and viewer. When you adapt content across platforms, you're not just reformatting; you're translating between those languages. And translation always loses something.

The most common losses when adapting:

- **Context compression:** A 10-minute YouTube explainer that earns its product mention through 8 minutes of trust-building loses that scaffolding when compressed to 45 seconds. The product mention that felt earned now feels abrupt.
- **Sound-dependent moments:** A reaction shot that lands because of the sound cue becomes a confusing pause on sound-off Instagram. Any content where the audio carries meaning — a surprised response, a dramatic pause filled by music, a punchline timed to a beat — needs to be rebuilt, not just reformatted.
- **Platform-specific behaviors:** TikTok's "POV:" and "stitch this" framings are native to that platform. On LinkedIn, they read as unprofessional. On Instagram Reels, they read as TikTok reposts.

To minimize translation loss: identify the 1-2 elements that make the original work, and protect those at all costs during adaptation. Everything else can be rebuilt for the target platform.

## When NOT to Repurpose

Not every script should be adapted. Repurpose only when the core message can survive the platform switch. Walk away when:

- **The hook is inseparable from the format.** A TikTok built around a trending audio, a "day in my life" vlog format, or a duet reaction has hooks that only work in that context. Stripping them out leaves nothing.
- **The content is too platform-specific to generalize.** A LinkedIn post about enterprise procurement won't translate to TikTok — the audience, vocabulary, and decision-making context are too different.
- **The source script is already underperforming.** Repurposing a weak script spreads the weakness. Fix the original first.
- **The tone gap is too wide.** A casual, low-production TikTok will feel cheap if directly adapted to LinkedIn, where production quality signals professionalism. The gap isn't just stylistic — it affects how the brand is perceived.

The test: if you can write the 1-sentence core message and it still holds up for the target audience on the target platform, repurpose. If it doesn't, create a derivative piece with a new angle instead.

## The 3-Second Hook Rewrite for Platform Switching

The same hook almost never works across platforms. The reasons are structural:

- **TikTok hooks** work on pattern interruption, curiosity gaps, and fast visual cuts. They assume the viewer is mid-scroll and need to stop them cold.
- **Instagram Reels hooks** lean more aesthetic and aspirational — the cover frame does work before the video even starts.
- **YouTube Shorts hooks** can afford 1-2 more seconds because viewer intent is slightly higher — people come to YouTube to watch, not just to scroll.
- **LinkedIn hooks** need to lead with outcome or professional credibility to earn the view from a work-context audience.

When rewriting a hook for a new platform, don't start with the words — start with the question: "What's the first thing this audience needs to feel in the first 3 seconds to keep watching?" Then write to that feeling, using the platform's native vocabulary.

## Caption-First Rewriting for Sound-Off Platforms

Instagram and LinkedIn are majority sound-off environments. Any script adaptation for these platforms needs to be rebuilt from the caption inward — not the other way around.

The caption-first approach:
1. Write the caption as if it's the only thing the viewer will read. It should carry the full message.
2. Treat the video as visual support for the caption — a demonstration, a face, a scene — not the primary message delivery vehicle.
3. On-screen text should mirror or reinforce caption language, not introduce new information.
4. Sound-dependent moments (reactions, music timing, verbal punchlines) need visual equivalents: text overlays, facial expressions timed to on-screen text, or cut-to moments that read without audio.

The test: mute the video and read only the caption and on-screen text. If the message lands, the adaptation works sound-off.

## Maintaining Brand Voice Across Platform Adaptations

Brand voice is not a script — it's a set of consistent choices about personality, vocabulary, and relationship to the audience. Those choices travel across platforms even when format and tone shift dramatically.

To maintain brand voice across adaptations:
- **Identify 3-4 brand voice markers** from the original brief (e.g., "direct without being blunt," "uses real customer language, not marketing speak," "never self-congratulatory").
- **Check each adaptation against those markers** before delivery. If the LinkedIn version sounds like a press release and the brand is conversational, the adaptation failed.
- **Vocabulary is the most portable element.** The specific words a brand uses — the way they describe their product, their customer, their outcome — should carry over even when sentence structure changes completely.
- **Tone can shift by platform without losing voice.** A brand can be "warmer and faster" on TikTok and "more composed" on LinkedIn while still sounding like the same brand. The mistake is letting the platform's native tone completely overwrite the brand's native tone.
