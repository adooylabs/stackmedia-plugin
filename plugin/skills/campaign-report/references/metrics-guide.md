# Campaign Metrics Guide

## Platform-Specific Benchmark Metrics

Understanding what "good" looks like on each platform is essential for honest reporting. These benchmarks are industry baselines — campaign-specific factors (niche audience, new brand, cold traffic) can shift them significantly.

**TikTok**
- CTR (link-in-bio / CTA): 1–3% is solid; above 3% is strong for direct response
- View-through rate (VTR): 25%+ at the halfway point indicates the hook held attention; 40%+ is excellent
- Engagement rate: 4–8% is average; 10%+ indicates high resonance
- Average watch time: aim for 50%+ of total video length

**Instagram Reels**
- Engagement rate: 3–6% is average; 7%+ is strong for branded content
- Reach rate (reach / followers): 20–40% for Reels vs. 5–10% for static posts
- Save rate: 1–2% signals high-value content worth benchmarking
- Story swipe-up / link CTR: 0.5–1.5% is typical; above 2% is strong

**YouTube (Long-Form)**
- Audience retention at midpoint: 40%+ is healthy; 50%+ is strong
- CTR (thumbnail to view): 2–5% is typical; 7%+ is excellent
- Average view duration: depends on video length — aim for 50% retention across the full video
- Subscriber conversion rate: 0.5–2% of viewers subscribing per video is solid for informational content

**YouTube Shorts**
- VTR (full view): 70%+ indicates strong hook and pacing
- CTR: 1–3% for channel/subscribe actions
- Engagement: comparable to TikTok benchmarks given format similarity

**LinkedIn**
- Engagement rate: 2–4% is average for video; 5%+ is strong
- Video view rate: 15–25% of impressions reaching the midpoint
- Comment rate: 0.5–1% signals professional resonance worth noting in reports

## Contextualizing Results vs. Industry Benchmarks

Raw benchmarks are starting points, not verdicts. When comparing campaign results to benchmarks, always contextualize:

- **Audience temperature:** Cold traffic (paid ads, new creators) consistently underperforms warm audiences. A 1.2% CTR on cold TikTok traffic may outperform a 2.5% CTR on a brand's existing audience in real conversion value.
- **Niche multipliers:** Highly specific audiences (B2B SaaS, medical, financial) typically show lower volume metrics but higher conversion intent. Don't penalize niche campaigns for not hitting mass-market engagement benchmarks.
- **Creative maturity:** First-time UGC campaigns often underperform benchmarks as the brand finds its creative voice. Frame this as baseline data, not failure.
- **Platform algorithm timing:** A campaign launching during a platform algorithm shift or against a major trend cycle will show different baseline performance. Flag this in reporting.

## Vanity Metrics vs. Performance Metrics

The clearest way to lose client trust is to lead a report with vanity metrics when the campaign goal was performance. Know the difference:

**Vanity metrics** (volume signals with limited business context):
- Total views / impressions
- Follower gains
- Total likes / comments

**Performance metrics** (tied to campaign objectives):
- CTR (link clicks / views) — are people taking action?
- Conversion rate (purchases, sign-ups / link clicks) — is action turning into business results?
- Cost per acquisition / CPL — what's the efficiency of spend?
- View-through rate — is the creative holding attention past the hook?

Always lead with the metric that matches the campaign goal. If the goal was brand awareness, views and reach are valid leads. If the goal was conversions, CTR and conversion rate are the story — and a high view count with low CTR is underperformance, not a win.

## Writing Honest Underperformance Analysis

Clients don't need protection from bad news — they need honest analysis that helps them make better decisions next time. Sugarcoating underperformance damages trust more than the underperformance itself.

How to frame underperformance without damaging the relationship:
- **Own the diagnosis:** "The hook format we tested — [specific format] — did not hold attention past the 3-second mark. VTR at 5 seconds was 18%, well below the 30% threshold."
- **Separate creative from platform:** Was it the script, the creator, the platform, or the targeting? Be specific about the root cause rather than letting the client assume it's everything.
- **Pair every underperformance finding with a hypothesis:** "We believe the problem was [X] because [evidence]. Testing [Y] in the next campaign would confirm or refute this."
- **Never blame the platform or algorithm without evidence.** "TikTok just wasn't working for us" is not analysis. "Our VTR dropped significantly after the first 3 seconds, suggesting the hook wasn't stopping the scroll — this is a creative issue, not a distribution issue" is.

## The Campaign Learning Frame

Even campaigns that miss KPIs generate data worth reporting. Framing underperformance as learning is not spin — it's accurate, and it reorients the client toward the next campaign rather than dwelling on the current one.

For every campaign, identify 2-3 learnings that have direct implications for what to test or change next:

- "Problem/solution hooks outperformed testimonial hooks 3:1 in CTR — the next campaign should weight problem/solution hooks more heavily."
- "Creator A's audience showed significantly higher conversion intent than Creator B's despite lower view counts — rebook Creator A and profile their audience for future casting."
- "The 45-second format outperformed the 60-second format on TikTok across all variations — optimize for shorter in the next brief."

Learnings are the most valuable deliverable in any campaign report. A campaign that missed its KPI but surfaced three actionable learnings has provided more long-term value than a campaign that hit KPIs but generated no insight into why.
