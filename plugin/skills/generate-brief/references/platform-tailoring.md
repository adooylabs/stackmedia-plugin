# Platform-Specific Brief Tailoring Guide

Use this reference when generating creative briefs for multi-platform campaigns. Each platform has distinct hook mechanics, content rhythms, and audience expectations. A brief that ignores platform context produces generic scripts.

---

## TikTok

**Audience mindset:** Entertainment-first. Discovery mode. Expects surprise and authenticity.

**Hook mechanics:**
- Verbal hooks dominate — a strong spoken line in the first 2 seconds beats a visual hook
- Best hooks are specific and unexpected: "I returned $400 of skincare after trying this"
- Questions work poorly; statements work better
- Identity-based hooks ("POV: you finally found something that works") outperform feature hooks

**Brief guidance:**
- Emphasize raw authenticity over polish — TikTok users penalize overly produced UGC
- Give creators permission to use their own words (brief should provide angle, not script)
- Include trend context: what hook formats are currently resonating on TikTok
- The "problem" section should be visceral and specific, not corporate

**Avoid in briefs:**
- "Showcase the product lifestyle" — too vague
- Multiple features — pick one
- Any instruction that would make the video look like an ad

---

## Instagram (Reels)

**Audience mindset:** Browse-mode, aspirational, community-oriented. More brand-aware than TikTok.

**Hook mechanics:**
- Visual hooks are equally as powerful as spoken hooks
- The cover frame (first frame) is a static image on the grid — design for both video and thumbnail
- Sound-off is default on Instagram — captions must carry the narrative fully
- Save-bait hooks outperform comment-bait: "Save this for your next order" > "Comment below"

**Brief guidance:**
- Include aesthetic direction (color palette, setting, styling notes)
- Brief should specify whether the content is Reels-first or Feed-first (or both)
- Aspirational lifestyle context is more important here than on TikTok
- Include explicit caption guidance — captions are part of the creative, not an afterthought

**Avoid in briefs:**
- Ignoring aesthetic direction — Instagram is the most visually judged platform
- Sound-dependent scripts with no caption alternative
- Generic "lifestyle" direction without specifics

## YouTube Shorts

**Audience mindset:** Search-intent adjacent. Slightly more information-seeking than TikTok. Rewards educational hooks.

**Hook mechanics:**
- The hook must work as both a spoken line AND an on-screen text statement (it may appear in search)
- Thumbnail frame (first frame) is a static image in YouTube search — plan for it
- Information density can be 30% higher than TikTok; the audience expects to learn something
- Subscribe-prompting CTAs work better here than on other short-form platforms

**Brief guidance:**
- Emphasize "what will they learn?" as the hook frame — curiosity + information promise
- Include thumbnail concept direction (what the first frame should look like as a static image)
- Note that Shorts content can be clipped from long-form YouTube for efficiency
- SEO keywords are relevant — brief should include 3-5 keyword targets

**Avoid in briefs:**
- Pure entertainment hooks with no information promise
- Ignoring the thumbnail frame
- Scripts that don't translate to a static first-frame

## YouTube Long-Form

**Audience mindset:** High intent, research mode. Willing to invest time. Trust is the currency.

**Hook mechanics:**
- The hook must make a promise the video keeps — "In the next 5 minutes, you'll know exactly how to X"
- Transformation and before/after hooks work extremely well
- Myth-bust hooks establish authority quickly
- The first 30 seconds must earn the next 2 minutes

**Brief guidance:**
- Include a recommended chapter structure (viewers expect chapters at this length)
- Emphasize objection handling as a required section — it's what builds trust at this length
- Include SEO metadata direction: title formulas, description keywords, tags
- Note that long-form can be repurposed into 3-4 Shorts clips (brief should identify those moments)
- Brand integration should feel earned, not forced — the creator's credibility is the product

**Avoid in briefs:**
- Over-scripting — long-form works best when creators tell their own story within a structure
- Feature lists — the brief should tell a story, not a spec sheet
- Missing the objection handling section

## LinkedIn

**Audience mindset:** Professional context. Scrolling in work mode. High skepticism for hype. Values credibility.

**Hook mechanics:**
- Business outcome leads: "We cut churn by 40% using this" stops a LinkedIn scroll
- Job title specificity: "If you're a Head of Sales..." works better than "If you're struggling..."
- Contrarian takes work: "Everyone in [industry] is doing X wrong" earns attention
- Social proof leads: A recognizable company name in the first 3 seconds builds immediate trust

**Brief guidance:**
- Emphasize outcomes and ROI language over feature descriptions
- Include targeting context (job title, company size, industry) — it should influence script tone
- LinkedIn is sound-off by default — captions are critical; brief should say this explicitly
- The "ask" should be small: "Watch this 2-minute demo" not "Book a 30-minute call"
- B2B buying is a team decision — brief should speak to the decision-maker's need to justify the choice

**Avoid in briefs:**
- Hype language ("revolutionary", "game-changing") — it reduces trust on LinkedIn
- Consumer-style authenticity hooks — too casual for professional context
- Hard CTAs — LinkedIn audiences need more nurturing before a sales ask
