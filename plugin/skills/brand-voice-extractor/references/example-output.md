# Brand Voice Guide: FormFocus
**Extracted:** 2026-04-15
**Source materials used:** Brand brief, tagline, product positioning, audience description

---

## Voice in One Sentence

FormFocus sounds like a pragmatic small business owner who figured out the hard way that simple tools beat powerful ones — not a software company that needs you to be impressed.

---

## Voice Pillars

### 1. Practical Over Polished
FormFocus communicates like someone who has actually run a small business and knows that time spent configuring a form is time not spent on customers. Every piece of copy earns its place by being useful first. There's no room for flourishes that don't move someone closer to solving their problem.

- **Sounds Like:** "Your intake form, done in 10 minutes. We timed it." / "Skip the tutorial. You'll figure it out."
- **Doesn't Sound Like:** "Empower your business workflow with our intuitive, enterprise-grade form solution." / "Unlock your team's full potential."

### 2. Quietly Confident
FormFocus doesn't shout about what it can do. It states facts, lets the product demonstrate itself, and trusts that smart people will draw the right conclusions. Confidence here means not over-explaining, not stacking adjectives, and not hedging with qualifiers.

- **Sounds Like:** "It works. Here's proof." / "Other form builders make you read a help doc. We don't."
- **Doesn't Sound Like:** "We believe we've created one of the most user-friendly form experiences available today." / "Our innovative approach is designed to potentially help you..."

### 3. Anti-Jargon by Default
FormFocus actively avoids the vocabulary of enterprise software, startup marketing, and tech influencer culture. This isn't a strategic choice — it's a values choice. The brand talks to people who are too busy to decode jargon, and respects that by not using any.

- **Sounds Like:** "Send a form. Get answers. Done." / "No setup fee. No learning curve. No nonsense."
- **Doesn't Sound Like:** "Leverage our API-first architecture to streamline your data capture workflows." / "Disrupt your lead gen funnel with AI-powered form intelligence."

### 4. Celebrates the Small Win
FormFocus understands that for a one-person business, getting a client intake form live before a Monday call is a real victory. The brand acknowledges these moments without being patronizing or saccharine. It's the tone of a peer who gets it — not a cheerleader trying to manufacture excitement.

- **Sounds Like:** "You just saved yourself 45 minutes of back-and-forth email. Nice." / "One less thing on the list."
- **Doesn't Sound Like:** "Congratulations on taking this incredible step toward business success!" / "You're crushing it! 🎉"

## Vocabulary Guide

### Use
- Plain nouns over branded ones: "form", "question", "answer", "client", "response"
- Verbs that imply speed and ease: "send", "done", "set up", "collect", "get"
- Dry acknowledgment phrases: "we timed it", "no, really", "actually works", "turns out"
- Light self-deprecation: "not magic, just good design", "we're not fancy — just useful"
- Second-person direct address: "you", "your clients", "your team"
- Sentence openers: "Here's the thing.", "Turns out,", "You don't need to...", "Skip the..."

### Never Use
- Enterprise jargon: leverage, synergy, scalable, robust, streamline, empower, solution, ecosystem
- Startup hype: disrupt, transform, revolutionize, game-changing, next-gen, AI-powered (unless technically precise)
- Vague promises: "help you succeed", "take your business to the next level", "unlock potential"
- Passive voice in product descriptions
- Exclamation marks in product UI (acceptable sparingly in celebration moments only)
- Acronyms without immediate plain-language translation

### Sentence Structure Patterns
- Short declarative sentences as anchors: "It's a form builder. It's fast." (2-6 words before expanding)
- Em-dashes for dry asides: "No setup required — we mean it."
- Contrast structures: "Other tools do X. We do Y."
- Rule of three broken on purpose: "Simple. Fast. Done." (full stop, no elaboration)
- Questions used to name the problem, not to engage: "Tired of sending PDFs and waiting three days for a reply?"
- Parenthetical humor that earns its place: "(Yes, even the conditional logic.)"

## Tone by Context

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| UGC scripts | Conversational, peer-to-peer, lightly self-deprecating | "I used to send Word docs to new clients. I know. It was bad. Then I found this." |
| Creative briefs | Direct, practical, no filler | "Show the form being built in under 60 seconds. No voiceover needed — speed sells itself." |
| Ad headlines | Blunt, specific, benefit-first | "Client intake form in 10 minutes. We timed it." |
| Social captions | Dry, short, one payoff line | "Built a whole onboarding form during a podcast. Not the point of the podcast, but here we are." |
| Email subject lines | Curiosity via specificity, never clickbait | "Your next client form (already done)" / "The 8-question intake form that books calls" |
| Error messages / UX copy | Honest, human, never blame the user | "Something went sideways. Try again — it usually works the second time." |

## Quick Reference

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Name the specific problem before the solution | Lead with features or technology |
| Use numbers and time references ("3 minutes", "8 questions") | Make vague time claims ("quickly", "instantly") |
| Acknowledge what was annoying before FormFocus existed | Pretend the status quo was fine |
| Let humor land quietly — one dry line, no fanfare | Stack jokes or use emoji-heavy enthusiasm |
| Trust the reader to connect the dots | Over-explain the benefit |
| Write like the brand has used the product | Write like the brand is pitching to investors |

## Creator Briefing Note

FormFocus is a form builder for people who run small businesses — freelancers, consultants, one-person shops, teams of five. These are busy people who've wasted real hours on bad tools, and they're a little skeptical of anything that promises to change their lives. Your energy on camera should match that: you're not a hype person, you're someone who found a thing that actually works and you're telling a friend about it. Practical, a little dry, genuinely useful. If you find yourself sounding like an infomercial — slow down. FormFocus would rather you underplay it. The brand's humor is quiet: a raised eyebrow, not a punchline. The brand's confidence is earned: show the thing working, don't just say it works. What would make FormFocus wince: over-promising, enterprise buzzwords, fake excitement, or treating a client intake form like it's going to change civilization. It's a good, useful tool. That's enough. Let that be enough.
