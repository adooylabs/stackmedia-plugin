# Feedback Templates & Tone Guide

## Opening Lines by Round

### Round 1 — Encouraging + Specific
The goal is to land the feedback without deflating the creator's confidence. Lead with what's real before pivoting to what needs to change.

- "Thanks for getting this in — there's a solid foundation here. The [specific element] reads naturally and your delivery instinct on [moment] is exactly the vibe we're after. Here's what needs to land before we can approve:"
- "Good first pass. The structure is there and [specific strength] works well. A few things need sharpening before this is ready:"
- "This is close. I can see what you were going for with [specific choice] and that direction is right. The main thing blocking approval is [issue] — once that's addressed, we're in good shape."

Avoid Round 1 openers that feel corporate or distant: "Thank you for your submission" is cold. "This needs significant revision" as an opener kills momentum before the creator even reads the notes.

### Round 2 — Direct + Focused
By Round 2, the creator has already received context. Skip the re-intro and go straight to what's still unresolved.

- "Round 1 feedback addressed [X] — appreciate the revision. The hook issue is still the main blocker. Here's specifically what's not landing and what would fix it:"
- "Good progress on [what improved]. Two things still need to change before approval:"
- "The [element] is better. The remaining gap is [specific issue] — here's what that fix looks like in practice:"

### Round 3+ — Crisp and Clear
This is not the place for cushioning. The creator needs precision, not preamble.

- "Still blocking on [issue]. Specifically: [exact problem]. Fix: [exact direction]. Nothing else needs to change."
- "Two outstanding items from Round 2 are unresolved. Here they are clearly:"

## Phrases That Work vs. Phrases to Avoid

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "This isn't quite there yet" | "The hook opens with a generic format — here's what would be more specific" |
| "The energy feels off" | "The pacing in the first 5 seconds is too slow — the problem statement needs to land by second 3" |
| "Make it more authentic" | "The product mention at 0:22 sounds scripted — try saying it the way you'd tell a friend" |
| "This doesn't work" | "This doesn't work because [X] — try [Y] instead" |
| "Great job overall!" | "[Specific element] is working well because [specific reason]" |
| "Fix the CTA" | "The CTA says 'check the link' — replace with [specific action tied to campaign goal]" |

## Handling Repeated Mistakes

When a creator hasn't addressed Round 1 feedback, the instinct is to re-explain at length. Resist it. Instead:

1. **Name the pattern directly:** "The hook issue from Round 1 is still present. I want to make sure the direction was clear, so I'm going to be more specific this time."
2. **Give a concrete example:** Don't re-describe the problem abstractly. Show what the fix looks like: "Instead of [what they wrote], something like [directional example] would land."
3. **Ask a calibration question if it keeps happening:** "Did the Round 1 note on the hook make sense? I want to make sure we're aligned before you revise again." This surfaces whether it's a comprehension gap or a creative disagreement.

Do not assume the creator is being careless — Round 1 feedback is sometimes unclear or easy to misread. Give one more specific pass before escalating to a conversation.

## When the Work Is Genuinely Bad

Sometimes the submission is far off brief and there's no amount of spin that makes that feel like a win. Be honest without being discouraging:

- Don't manufacture positives. If the script doesn't work structurally, saying "great energy!" before listing six major blockers is condescending. Find one real thing that shows promise — even if it's just "the sign-off is solid" — or skip the positives section and lead with: "This isn't hitting the brief yet, and I want to be straight with you about why so we can get it there."
- Name what went wrong at the brief level: "This reads as a product review, but the brief called for a problem/solution narrative. That's the root of most of the issues here."
- Give a clear reset direction: "Rather than revising line by line, I'd recommend starting from the hook and rebuilding the structure. Here's the frame to work from: [direction]."
- Don't soften the round count: If this is Round 2 and the script is still off, say so. "This is further from the brief than the Round 1 submission" is harder to say but more useful than implying progress was made when it wasn't.

The goal is honesty in service of the creator succeeding — not honesty as a blunt instrument. Frame bad news as solvable, give a clear path forward, and trust the creator to handle direct feedback professionally.
