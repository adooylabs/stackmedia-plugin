# Extended B2B Scripting Principles for LinkedIn

This reference goes deeper than the 8 principles in SKILL.md. Use these when crafting scripts that need to resonate with professional audiences, navigate multi-stakeholder decisions, or perform within LinkedIn's algorithm.

---

## The Trust Hierarchy on LinkedIn

LinkedIn audiences evaluate content through a layered trust filter:

1. **Personal credibility** — Does this person seem like a real practitioner? Have they done the work they're talking about? A founder, ops manager, or department head speaking from experience carries more weight than a polished brand spokesperson.
2. **Company brand** — Is this a company I've heard of, or one my peers use? Brand recognition provides social permission to keep watching.
3. **Product features** — Only after trust is established does the viewer care what the product actually does.

Scripts that lead with product features skip the first two levels and lose the audience before the hook lands. Lead with the person and their credibility, then the problem, then the product.

## Decision Committee Dynamics

In B2B, the LinkedIn viewer is rarely the sole decision-maker. Your script may be watched by:

- **The champion** — Wants to buy, needs ammunition to bring to their manager
- **The skeptic** — Their colleague forwarded it; they're looking for reasons to say no
- **The evaluator** — On a shortlist research mission; comparing 3-4 tools
- **The passive scroller** — Not actively looking, but will file it away if it's relevant

**Implication for scripting:** Build in a "why I chose this" or "what convinced me" moment. This gives internal champions language to use in their next team meeting. "The thing that sold me was..." is a sentence the viewer can repeat. "FormFocus lets you build forms faster" is not.

Don't script for the buyer alone. Script for the internal pitch they'll make after they watch.

## Content Format Matching

Not every LinkedIn video needs to be a 30-second sponsored ad. Match the format to the intent:

| Format | Best For | Typical Length |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| Sponsored Ad | Direct response, demo requests, lead gen | 15-30 seconds |
| Thought Leadership | Brand awareness, trust building, organic reach | 2-5 minutes |
| Document Post | Step-by-step guides, frameworks, listicles | Carousel PDF |
| Native Video (organic) | Founder stories, team culture, product walkthroughs | 60-90 seconds |

Sponsored ads prioritize conversion — tight hook, clear value, one CTA. Thought leadership prioritizes credibility — the product mention is proportionate (roughly 20-30% of the total runtime), not the centerpiece.

Mismatching format to intent is one of the most common B2B LinkedIn errors: a 2-minute video with a hard "book a demo" CTA every 30 seconds feels like a pitch deck, not a conversation.

## LinkedIn Algorithm Signals

LinkedIn's feed ranking weights the following signals (roughly in order of importance):

1. **Comments** — LinkedIn heavily weights comment engagement over likes. A video with 12 comments outperforms one with 150 likes. Scripts should end with a question or prompt that invites professional debate: "What's your take?" or "Has anyone else dealt with this?" not just "Learn more."
2. **Dwell time** — How long a viewer watches before scrolling. Hooks that create genuine curiosity (not clickbait) extend dwell time. The algorithm rewards videos where viewers watch past the 50% mark.
3. **Reposts** — Signal that the content has professional value worth sharing. Thought leadership scripts should include at least one quotable line — something a viewer would want to repost with their own commentary.
4. **Saves** — LinkedIn users save content they intend to act on later. Scripts that include a "here's a framework" or "here's how I think about this" moment encourage saves.
5. **Early engagement velocity** — The first 60 minutes after posting determine reach. For sponsored content, this is less relevant (paid distribution), but for organic thought leadership, posting time and initial seeding matter.

## Caption Strategy

The LinkedIn caption is underestimated. It is a second hook — the first line appears in the feed before the video auto-plays, and before "...see more" cuts off the rest.

**Rules for the first 150 characters:**
- Open with the strongest line, not a preamble ("In this video I'll be sharing..." wastes the hook)
- Make it standalone — assume the viewer reads the caption without watching the video
- Ask a question, make a claim, or state a surprising fact
- No external links in the caption body — LinkedIn suppresses reach for posts with outbound links. Put the link in the first comment.

**Example (bad):** "Excited to share this new video about how FormFocus helped one of our clients streamline their onboarding process. Check it out!"

**Example (good):** "We were spending 6 hours a week manually routing form submissions. This is what we changed."

The caption should feel like something a real professional wrote, not a marketing department.

## Professional Video Production Notes

LinkedIn is a more polished environment than TikTok or Instagram Reels. Production quality signals credibility to a professional audience:

- **Audio is non-negotiable** — Bad audio kills B2B video faster than any other production flaw. A $30 lavalier mic outperforms any camera upgrade. LinkedIn viewers are often watching in environments where they can hear clearly (desk, headphones, commute).
- **Setting matters** — Office environments, home offices, and workspace settings all perform well. Consumer-lifestyle settings (coffee shop aesthetics, gym backgrounds) can feel off-brand for B2B content unless the brand specifically plays in that space.
- **Eye contact and framing** — Direct-to-camera, centered, with the creator filling roughly 60% of the frame. LinkedIn viewers respond to direct address.
- **Text overlays** — Essential. Assume 50-60% of viewers watch without sound. Key claims, statistics, and the CTA should all appear as captions or text overlays.
- **Energy calibration** — LinkedIn energy sits between YouTube vlog energy (relaxed) and a conference talk (formal). It's a professional peer conversation, not a sales pitch and not a casual chat. Think: "how you'd explain this to a smart colleague over coffee."

## A/B Testing Guidance for LinkedIn

When running paid LinkedIn campaigns, structured testing accelerates learning:

**What to test first:**
1. **Hook variation** — Test 2 hooks against the same body: one Business Outcome hook vs. one Pain Point hook. This is highest-impact because LinkedIn users decide in 3 seconds.
2. **CTA variation** — "See a 2-minute demo" vs. "Download the guide" vs. "Watch the full case study." Soft CTAs often outperform hard CTAs on LinkedIn.
3. **Headline variation** — The text headline below the video in a sponsored post has significant impact on click-through rate. Test question vs. statement.

**What not to test simultaneously:** Avoid changing the hook AND the CTA in the same test. You won't know which variable drove the result.

**Targeting overlap caution:** LinkedIn's audience targeting can create overlap between campaigns if you're running multiple variations to similar audiences. Use LinkedIn's Audience Expansion controls carefully, and consider separating test audiences by company size or seniority tier to avoid cannibalization.

**Minimum runtime for signal:** Run LinkedIn video ad tests for at least 7-10 days before reading results. LinkedIn's auction dynamics mean early performance can be noisy, especially for smaller budgets.
