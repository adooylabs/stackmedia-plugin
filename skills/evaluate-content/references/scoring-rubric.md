# Scoring Rubric — Detailed Reference

This document expands on the 7 evaluation criteria with concrete score examples, common failure modes, and over-scoring traps. Use this as your calibration reference when scores feel uncertain.

---

## 1. Hook Strength

The hook is the first 1–3 seconds (spoken or visual). It is the only part of the script that competes directly with infinite scroll. It either stops the viewer or it doesn't.

**Score 9 example:** The creator opens mid-sentence, slightly out of breath: "Okay wait — I just saved 40 minutes on something I do every single morning and I'm genuinely annoyed nobody told me sooner." No setup. No "hi guys." The viewer is already inside a story they didn't choose to enter.

**Score 6 example:** "If you run a small business, you know how much time admin takes. Today I want to talk about something that changed my workflow." Competent. Clear. Absolutely scroll-pastable. The promise is real but the delivery is flat and the format is familiar.

**Score 2 example:** "Hey everyone! Welcome back to my channel. Today I'm partnering with [Brand] to show you their new tool." This is the format TikTok's algorithm was designed to kill. No tension, no open loop, immediate disclosure of the ad format.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- Opening with a greeting ("Hey guys," "Welcome back")
- Starting with a question that has an obvious or uninteresting answer ("Do you ever feel stressed?")
- Leading with the brand name before establishing any tension or curiosity
- Using a hook format that was oversaturated 6 months ago (POV format, "nobody talks about this," "I tried X so you don't have to" without specificity)
- Hooks that are visually inert — nothing happening in the frame while the creator talks at the camera

**Over-scoring trap:** Evaluators often give 7s to hooks that are merely "not bad." If the hook doesn't create genuine forward momentum — a reason the viewer must keep watching — it hasn't earned above a 6. Ask yourself: would you personally stop scrolling on this? If the honest answer is "probably not," that's a 5 or 6, not a 7.

## 2. Storytelling

A UGC script's story arc should feel like something that actually happened to a real person — problem encountered, solution discovered, outcome achieved. The best arcs have specificity: real numbers, real moments, real friction before the resolution.

**Score 9 example:** The creator describes a specific morning — 7:43am, client intake forms, three back-and-forth emails before even opening the project. The frustration is granular. The moment they found the solution is a real moment with a real reaction ("I actually laughed because it was so obvious"). The outcome is specific ("I got that hour back every single day, which for me is about $200 in recovered time").

**Score 6 example:** "I used to struggle with client onboarding. It took forever and stressed me out. Then I found this tool and now it's so much easier." The arc exists but it's generic. The problem and solution are both stated, not shown. A viewer can follow it but won't feel it.

**Score 2 example:** The script opens with a product description, pivots to a list of features, ends with a call to action. There is no arc. There is no person in the story. It reads as a product spec read aloud.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- Problem stated in one sentence, solution in one sentence — no breathing room or emotional texture
- The creator's role in the story is passive ("I heard about this tool") rather than experiential ("I was 20 minutes into my third failed attempt when...")
- Resolution appears before the problem has been fully established — the stakes never build
- The story is implausible given the product category (overpromising in the arc undermines authenticity)
- No emotional beat — the viewer never has a reason to care

**Over-scoring trap:** Scripts with a technically correct three-act structure (problem → discovery → resolution) often get 7s they don't deserve. Structure is table stakes. The question is whether the arc has emotional weight. A story that follows the formula without specificity or feeling is a 5, not a 7.

## 3. Script Naturalness

Real creators don't speak in complete sentences. They trail off, use filler phrases as personality markers, reference things off-camera, and have verbal tics. A natural script sounds like it was spoken first and written down second.

**Score 9 example:** "Okay so — and I know this sounds dramatic — but this genuinely changed how I start my day. Like, I used to just dread opening my laptop." Short sentences. Incomplete thoughts. "Okay so" as a genuine opener. The "I know this sounds dramatic" creates a relatable self-awareness. A creator could deliver this without a single read-through.

**Score 6 example:** "I've been using this tool for three weeks now, and I have to say, it's made a significant difference in how I manage my client workflow." Grammatically correct. Every clause resolved. Reads like a testimonial written by a copywriter. A creator would need to deliberately rough this up to make it land.

**Score 2 example:** "FormFocus is the industry-leading client intake solution that streamlines your onboarding process through automated workflows and intelligent form routing." This is product marketing copy. It cannot be delivered naturally by a human being. It would require a voiceover, not a creator.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- Full, formal sentences where fragments are more natural
- No contractions ("I am" instead of "I'm," "you will" instead of "you'll")
- Transition phrases that only exist in writing ("Furthermore," "In addition to this," "As a result")
- Passive voice constructions that no one uses in speech
- Jargon that the persona wouldn't actually use ("streamline," "optimize," "leverage")

**Over-scoring trap:** Evaluators often give naturalism credit for first-person voice and colloquial vocabulary, even when sentence structure remains formally written. A script full of "honestly" and "literally" can still read as scripted if the underlying cadence is wrong. Test by reading it aloud — if you stumble even once, the naturalness score should drop.

## 4. Production Feasibility

UGC creators are solo operators. Their setup is typically a smartphone on a tripod or handheld, a ring light or window light, their home or a simple location, and basic props. Scripts that require anything beyond this impose real cost and friction.

**Score 9 example:** All shots are from a single location (home office). Visual directions are specific but simple: "hold up phone showing the form screen," "cut to over-shoulder view of laptop," "back to talking head for CTA." Everything on the list is achievable in an afternoon with no additional equipment or coordination.

**Score 6 example:** The script calls for three different locations (home, coffee shop, walking outside), a screen recording insert, and a product flat lay. Technically achievable, but it's a half-day shoot minimum. A creator charging $200–300 per video will likely cut corners, which affects final quality.

**Score 2 example:** The script calls for a split-screen comparison showing two workflows simultaneously, a motion graphic overlay explaining the product architecture, and a "before/after" sequence requiring two separate sets. This is a production brief, not a UGC script.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- B-roll requirements that assume a separate camera operator
- Screen recording inserts without guidance on how to integrate them (most TikTok creators don't edit in complex cutaways)
- Props that require advance ordering or significant styling
- Lighting requirements that assume a controlled studio environment
- Location assumptions (professional office, kitchen that looks "aspirational") that don't match how most creators live

**Over-scoring trap:** Scripts that are technically feasible but overcomplicated often get 8s because "a creator could pull it off." Feasibility is about what a creator will actually do under normal production conditions, not theoretical maximum effort. If a script requires more than 2 location setups or more than 3 distinct visual elements, score conservatively.

## 5. Brand Integration

The product should appear in the script as a natural plot point — something the creator found, tried, or uses — not as an insertion. Weak brand integration breaks the UGC illusion by making the commercial intent obvious.

**Score 9 example:** The product is introduced at the exact moment in the story where the creator found it — after the problem has been established and the viewer is ready to receive a solution. The features mentioned match the specific pain points raised earlier in the script. The brand name is used once naturally, not repeated for awareness frequency.

**Score 6 example:** The product is mentioned but the transition feels abrupt ("So that's when I decided to try FormFocus — a client intake tool that..."). The story pauses to explain the product rather than continuing through it. The features listed don't map directly to the pains raised in the hook.

**Score 2 example:** The creator's problem in the hook is stress, but the product solution is productivity. The brief called for targeting freelancers but the script references "your team." The product name is used four times in 45 seconds. The CTA is a direct sales line ("Use code X for 20% off") that drops out of the narrative entirely.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- Product introduced before the problem is fully established
- Features listed as bullet points rather than woven into story moments
- Brand name used so frequently it signals "this is an ad" to the algorithm and the viewer
- Claims that don't match what the brief approved (making up benefits not in the brief)
- CTA that breaks the fourth wall in a jarring way

**Over-scoring trap:** Scripts get inflated Brand Integration scores when the product is "naturally mentioned" but the mention doesn't advance the story or connect to specific brief claims. Natural placement is necessary but not sufficient. The integration should feel inevitable — like the story couldn't have been told without this product.

## 6. Platform Awareness

Each platform has a distinct content grammar. TikTok rewards speed and pattern interrupts. Instagram Reels rewards polish and aspirational framing. YouTube Shorts rewards looping hooks and quick cuts. LinkedIn rewards authority and insight. A script that ignores this grammar feels out of place even if the content is good.

**Score 9 example (TikTok):** The script opens at the problem with no setup. Total runtime is 28–35 seconds. There are two or three natural cut points built into the visual directions. The CTA is embedded in the story ("link in bio" said as part of a natural thought) rather than appended. The pacing assumes a viewer who will tap away in 2 seconds if they're bored.

**Score 6 example:** The script is paced for a 60-second Instagram Reel but is being targeted at TikTok. It's well-structured but 20 seconds too long. The hook is soft by TikTok standards. A viewer on TikTok would be gone by second 8.

**Score 2 example:** The script is 90 seconds, formally structured, includes a title card direction, and ends with a multi-line CTA. It could be a YouTube ad or a LinkedIn post. There is nothing in the script that reflects TikTok-specific best practices, pacing, or culture.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- Wrong runtime for the platform (TikTok scripts over 60 seconds, LinkedIn scripts under 45 seconds)
- Pacing that doesn't match platform norms (LinkedIn audiences expect more deliberate delivery; TikTok audiences expect faster cuts)
- CTA format wrong for platform (swipe-up language on TikTok, "link in bio" on a platform with clickable links)
- Missing platform-specific visual conventions (talking head dominates TikTok; carousels dominate Instagram education content)
- Ignoring current platform trends in favor of generic formats

**Over-scoring trap:** Scripts that meet the basic format requirements (right length, first-person voice) often get 7s for platform awareness when they haven't actually internalized platform culture. Being the right length is not platform awareness — it's the minimum. Platform awareness means the script reads like it was conceived for that specific platform, not adapted to fit.

## 7. Creative Direction

Creative direction is the angle — the lens through which the story is told. The same product, the same target persona, and the same brief can yield dozens of different creative angles. Weak creative direction picks the most obvious one. Strong creative direction finds a perspective that feels fresh.

**Score 9 example:** Instead of the expected "I was overwhelmed by my workflow" angle, the creator opens with the moment they almost quit freelancing — and frames the product as the thing that changed the math on whether the business was worth keeping. The emotional stakes are higher. The angle is unexpected. No competitor would have produced this same script.

**Score 6 example:** The script uses a reliable "before/after" structure. The pain point is relatable. The solution is clear. Nothing about the angle is wrong, but nothing about it is memorable either. A viewer who has seen 10 UGC ads in this category would recognize the template immediately.

**Score 2 example:** The script opens with "Are you tired of wasting time on [common pain point]?" — a format that has been used in an estimated 40% of productivity app UGC. The angle is the category default. There is no creative ownership. A different product could be inserted with minimal changes.

**Common failure modes (1–4):**
- Using the first creative angle that comes to mind without exploring alternatives
- Relying on category-default framings (productivity = time savings, fitness = transformation, finance = stress reduction)
- Hooks borrowed from oversaturated TikTok formats with no adaptation to the specific brand
- Emotional territory that's too safe — relatable but not resonant
- No point of view from the creator — the script could have been written for any creator, not this one

**Over-scoring trap:** Scripts with a distinct visual direction (unusual opening shot, interesting location) often receive creative direction credit that belongs to production, not writing. Creative direction is about the conceptual angle — the "why this story, told this way" — not visual style. A visually interesting script with a generic narrative angle is still a 5 or 6 on creative direction.
