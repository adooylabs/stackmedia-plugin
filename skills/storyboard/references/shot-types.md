# Shot Type Reference

Complete shot type definitions with when-to-use guidance for UGC and video ad production.

---

## Core Shot Types

### ECU — Extreme Close-Up
**Frame:** Single product detail, eye, fingertip, texture, logo  
**When to use:** High-impact reveals, product quality proof, emotional peak moments. Use sparingly — the tighter the shot, the higher the expectation of payoff. Best for: unboxing details, ingredient labels, skin texture before/after.

### CU — Close-Up
**Frame:** Face fills the frame (chin to forehead) or product is the dominant subject  
**When to use:** Emotional connection, authenticity, reaction shots. The workhorse of UGC. Viewers trust faces they can read. Use for spoken hooks, confessional moments, and "this is the thing" product reveals.

### MCU — Medium Close-Up
**Frame:** Top of chest to top of head  
**When to use:** Conversational talking-head content. Most TikTok and Reels content lives here. Gives creators room to gesture slightly. Feels natural and relatable — not too intimate, not too distant.

### MS — Medium Shot
**Frame:** Waist to top of head  
**When to use:** When body language matters — gesturing to a product, walking into frame, demonstrating something at waist or chest height. Provides environmental context without committing to a full wide.

### WS — Wide Shot
**Frame:** Full body plus meaningful environment  
**When to use:** Scene establishment, location context, lifestyle shots that show the creator "in their world." Use at the top of a new scene location or when the setting itself is part of the story.

### POV — Point of View
**Frame:** What the creator sees from their eyes  
**When to use:** Immersive demonstrations, product interactions (opening an app, unboxing, applying a product). Creates "you are there" intimacy. Particularly effective for app/software demos and step-by-step tutorials.

### OTS — Over-the-Shoulder
**Frame:** Camera positioned behind and beside the creator, looking past their shoulder at the subject  
**When to use:** When reacting to something (a screen, a product, another person). Adds perspective and depth. Works well for reaction content and side-by-side comparison moments.

### Insert
**Frame:** Isolated product or object shot, typically tightly framed  
**When to use:** Product demos, feature highlights, ingredient callouts. The insert is the "evidence" shot — it shows what the spoken line is claiming. Every product claim benefits from an insert to back it up.

## Additional Shot Types

### Aerial / Drone
**Frame:** Looking down from above, wide or medium  
**When to use:** Lifestyle context, location establishing shots, aspirational lifestyle brands. Rarely used in UGC unless the location IS the product (travel, outdoor, real estate). Requires drone equipment or stock footage.

### Two-Shot
**Frame:** Two subjects in frame simultaneously  
**When to use:** Testimonial duos, creator + product side-by-side, before/after with two creators. Useful for reaction and validation content ("I had my friend try it too").

### Reaction Shot
**Frame:** CU or MCU capturing a subject's emotional response, typically immediately after something happens  
**When to use:** After a product reveal, after applying/using/tasting the product, after reading results. The reaction shot IS the conversion moment — it answers "does this actually work?" before the viewer has to ask.

### B-Roll
**Frame:** Supporting footage that covers a spoken line or transition without a talking head  
**When to use:** Everywhere the creator doesn't need to be on screen — lifestyle shots, product in use, environment details. B-roll elevates production quality and gives editors flexibility. Plan at least 2-3 B-roll moments per 30 seconds of content.

## Camera Movement Vocabulary

| Movement | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Static** | Camera does not move | Talking heads, product reveals, CTA frames — stability = authority |
| **Pan** | Horizontal pivot on a fixed axis (left or right) | Establishing shots, following movement, revealing something off-screen |
| **Tilt** | Vertical pivot on a fixed axis (up or down) | Revealing height, scanning a product top-to-bottom, dramatic reveals |
| **Zoom** | Optical or digital magnification change | Emphasis on a detail, dramatic build-up — use sparingly, digital zoom degrades quality |
| **Dolly** | Camera physically moves forward or backward on a track or surface | Creates depth, professional feel, "push in" on an emotional moment |
| **Tracking Shot** | Camera moves alongside a moving subject | Following creator walking, dynamic lifestyle shots |
| **Handheld** | Camera held without stabilization, produces natural shake | Authentic, raw, documentary feel. Right for casual UGC hooks and relatable content |
| **Gimbal** | Handheld but electronically stabilized — smooth motion without tripod stiffness | Polished UGC, product walkthroughs, lifestyle brand content |

**Rule of thumb:** Handheld feels authentic. Gimbal feels polished. Static feels authoritative. Match the movement to the brand's desired perception.

## Transition Types

| Transition | Description | Best Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Cut** | Immediate switch from one shot to the next | Default for fast-paced content; every TikTok hook should open on a cut |
| **Dissolve** | Gradual blend between two shots | Softer emotional transitions, time passing, dream/memory sequences |
| **Fade** | Image fades to/from black or white | Scene endings, emotional weight, chapter breaks in long-form |
| **Wipe** | New shot pushes the old one off screen in a direction | Style-forward; trending on TikTok with left/right swipe effects |
| **Jump Cut** | Cut to a slightly different angle/position of the same subject | Fast-paced talking head, adds energy, common in tutorial content |
| **Match Cut** | Cut where a visual element (shape, movement, position) carries across the cut | Creates continuity, looks expensive on a small budget — plan these deliberately |
| **Whip Pan** | Very fast pan that blurs the frame, cutting on the blur to a new shot | High energy, trendy, effective for hooks and rapid transitions between points |
| **L-Cut** | Audio from the next scene begins before the visual cut | Creates seamless narrative flow; the voice leads the eye forward |
| **J-Cut** | Audio from the current scene continues into the next shot | Smooth pacing for emotional or story-driven content; feels cinematic |

## Platform-Specific Framing Notes

### TikTok / Instagram Reels / YouTube Shorts (9:16 Vertical)
- Keep all primary action in the **center safe zone** — roughly the middle 80% of the frame width
- Avoid placing subjects or text in the **top 15%** (UI overlays) or **bottom 20%** (caption area, CTA buttons)
- Frame faces at **upper-center** rather than dead-center — leave breathing room at top for text overlays
- Products should be **center-frame or slightly below center** to avoid being obscured by platform UI
- For two-shot compositions, stack subjects **vertically** rather than side-by-side

### YouTube Long-Form (16:9 Landscape)
- Apply the **rule of thirds** — place subjects at intersecting gridlines, not dead-center
- The **first frame** should be composable as a standalone thumbnail — strong subject, clear expression, readable composition
- Leave **headroom** (space above the subject's head) but not too much — 10-15% of frame height
- B-roll can use full 16:9 compositional freedom; talking heads should stay at thirds

### LinkedIn (1:1 Square)
- Frame talking heads with subject **centered**, from mid-chest to just above the head
- Keep backgrounds **clean and professional** — distracting backgrounds perform worse on LinkedIn
- Text overlays should stay in the **center 70%** of the frame
- For product shots, give the product **generous whitespace** — LinkedIn audiences respond to clarity over density
